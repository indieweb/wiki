== Notes ==
* Welcome to the session everyone!
** We have 30 mins for the session!
* Zegnat: when shares website, has lots of contact information, but not a lot of information about what he is doing for work or how to reach out professionally.
* There are people here who only have portfolios rather than personal websites.
* What would you like to add / change to your website in terms of personal / professional representation?
* Also free to share things you have done, found successful in the past!
* Joschi: tollwerk.de website is indie-webby; Joschi has put a lot of effort into that.
** Put more effort into org website rather than personal.
** Doesn't know of many/any company websites that are IndieWebified
* Tantek: site is more personal, neglected resume on website for ~10 years or more
** But he has a resume static page
* There are lots of examples of people who have separate resumes on their website
** Peter Molnar has a CV page on his website; marked up with h-resume
** Max has had a professional and personal website which were on different domains.
*** In the end, merged them together; it was too much of a hassle to have two different websites with their own styles. It turned out that the professional website wasn't as needed at the time so it was never updated.
* Grey area: things that aren't as relevant professionally
* Maybe you want to discuss sensitive topics like politics, etc. on your personal website
* AI could synthesize information
** How do we put hidden text on website to tell the AI to go away?
** Research published [citation needed] that said AI will prefer text written by AI
* Website is my self
** What if we could disable part of our website for a period of time, or let another part show more?
*** Maybe like adding links on website home page?
** You might not want to show something on a given day, but show it later -- a creative interpretation instead of binary
** What is the definition of professional?
*** Professional from an employee point of view, and in the case of a self-employed person trying to find clients
*** Employees care about employers, self-employed care about clients
*** What you communicate may be different depending on the audience
*** Showing capabilities of what you can do / have done 
*** How does your voice change depending on the audience?
* People's preferences on what they share on their website is different
** You can use your site to communicate what you do want in a job / vs. signal what you don't want
* Someone has a disclaimer on their website for what they don't want to do
** https://www.n2.studio/about#:~:text=i'm%20the%20wrong%20guy%20if%20you%3A
** Alternatively: you can be explicit about what you do want to do.
* James: if you make your website your primary identity on the web, and you only share your blog, as I change there is a voice in the back of my mind, that company I worked for 6 years ago, are they reading my blog now? Maybe they'll find an idea and join the indieweb(!)
** there's something about a degree of separation of another website, perhaps people who want to hire you freelance.
** some people like the example of gardening content appealing to colleagues
** Combining identities in a single place problem: what if you decide years later you only want a certain group to access your website?
*** Related: RSS-only posts
* Tantek: separate domains?
* Sebastian (https://make.sfeld.eu/): yes I do, recruiters say they search for certain things, lists of things in your portfolio, lots of keywords, you get calls from recruiters for stuff you will never do at work
** for me it is better to have separate professional and personal website, this is what I want to be paid for, and this is what I do for fun
** I find it hard to combine both.
* Startups / small companies look for generalists, larger enterprises look for specialists (?); debate going on in the room :)
* How people find your website, and where they land
** Could you have a button that says "For professional me, go here"...
** Where is the starting point where people land on?
** How will this change with AI?
* Joschi's website has a single view that's easy to comprehend
* One strategy for separation: using your "government name" for professional uses, and for personal website they have a pseudomous alias.
** Not easy in Germany because you need to put your address on your website to identify yourself
* You can design for the kind of person who you want to come to your website
** Make it more creative / text-heavy, etc. depending on the audience
** It can take a bit of time before someone explores a website more; you need to have a special intent to click further into the architecture of a website.
** Nora's website is a professional portfolio and also whimsical
*** Nora wouldn't want anyone to hire them who doesn't appreciate the whimsy on her website
* Professional and personal could be the same because they both represent you
** James too wouldn't want to be hired by someone who didn't acknowledge the significance of his website to him
* Personal skills like drawing fan art can show your creativity which may be appealing to employers
* Other person in the room: anyone who wants to hire someone will probably be happy looking through a personal site to find the professional one if you make it easy.
* Making web pages specifically for a client
** Could you have a custom resume with different links?
** It would be hard to make a page that lists everything you have done if you have been working for many years; filtering for a client would be ideal so they can see the most relevant links.
*** Related again: "what is a professional website?" -- everyone will have different meanings.
** medium-sized companies may look for different things to small companies, etc.
* In-URL filter, or let people filter themselves
* You could have short URLs with different pages
* You could make a subdomain like tollwerk.example.com that is tailored for reaching out to them
** Could be a good sign for agencies: you have a portfolio specifically for us
** Important: avoid enumeration
** Jakob when he applied here, made a site specifically for Tollwerk, but Tollwerk didn't check (but that's because Tollwerk knew him already)
* You could track page visits
* You could put random company names as Easter Eggs, like starfleet.example.com
** "Oh, they also applied to Google, maybe I should hire them..." :D
* What is the use for the professional website? -- applying to a specific company, applying to many companies, might be to have a professional site on the web for if someone searches for your website on Google, etc.
* How do you beat LinkedIn on search results
* There may be many people who have your name with whom you are competing
* If you have multiple websites there is a mental overhead
* Ranking for keywords could be a fun way to communicate your expertise on a topic (not authoritative, but fun)
** James' "taylor swift song acronyms" or "software maintenance blog"
* We'll have domain addicts anonymous later today -- it will not be recorded.
* Three domains: professional, private, cool. When last time they were updated: professional is up to date, cool website in Latin but it's not online.
* You might want to get your name [dot] com and keep it
** If you have a domain, keep it if it is your name in case it gets taken over by spammers, etc. 
** At minimum, redirect to your LinkedIn if you don't have the time to maintain content on your website.
* Does anyone feel any pressure to post on their website for professional audience?
** This is a thing on LinkedIn
*** Lots of satire around this
** "That's not who I am or want to be"

== Notes ==
* Guarding posts with IndieAuth as a way of identifying yourself
* Inviting friends to access a specific posts
* Reasons for posting privately
** Sharing thoughts and feelings that you don't want everyone to see
* State of the Browser has private posts for attendees
** Uses Netlify to do authentication but involves a lot of manual work (there are 400 people who need this); generated automatically.
* Jo used to have a wiki for school notes that had a .htaccess file that said the site was private
* Easier to use what already exists in terms of authentication instead of having to build a custom solution
* Different content for public vs private, like on social media accounts such as Instagram
* UK study "Fewer UK adults posting on social media, Ofcom finds": https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/cgqk718l4neo

* LLMs eat so much content and this could include your addresses that are on Imprint / Impressum (which obligate you to put a physical address on there)
** Using images as a getaround, but the law says that the impressum should be accessible which would require alt text
** LLMs can synthesise information which means that they can aggregate more than was previously possible

* When you have a website you need to consider that the information is more searchable, etc.
* You can use robots.txt to Disallow Google, Bing, etc.
** https://indieweb.org/robots_txt
* Privacy of in-jokes, mentioning people (are they ok with it, do you know them well enough)


* Anonymity when you are posting on your website
** You might want to set up a private blog with someone to talk about a mutual interest
** You could use someone's first initial which makes people less identifiable
*** But this is harder to find for people with less of a web presence.
** You could do a find and replace on people's names; if someone is logged in, the find and replace could work. A web component could help?
** Having information that is private unless someone is signed in
** Having a "private paragraph" with hidden information only seen on log in
** Blurring images by default / hiding images with faces until someone is logged in

* Issue with logged in work: you have to manage accounts, you have to decide audiences.
** You might have to create different user groups too
** Is most of the time: I don't want search engines, LLMs, etc.
** So you might want to make it "private for any person" as a mode and a public mode
* Logged in / not logged in boolean
* Managing accounts = more work, might discourage from writing
** https://indieweb.org/audience
** "Who do I want to see this post?"
** [[at-mention]]s could be a good way of inviting someone into a blog post
* Privacy and trust that users/visitors respect the privacy of the post
** showing clearly that something is supposed to be private
** Having some user interface affordance to say someone is logged in; a policy that says "hey, you're my friend"
** Share buttons should be disabled or altered
** If you're sharing a URL, it might not be clear to the person that the post is private?

* Additional security overhead of private posts
** responisbility of other peoples data, potentially, if accounts used
* Capability URLs; isn't linked anywhere but if someone links
* If your site is public on GitHub, you might need a private repo that is in a different place.
** Git submodules
* How to avoid accidentally publishing something publicly

* Things we'd want to keep private
** Swarm checkins
** Diaries
** Lettters to friends
** Access things that you have already made; upload more things like art and make it accessible when you need it.

* Consideration: silo profiles may be indexed by search engines.
** Strava has an option to obfuscate information / remove zones of information.
** Strava "public" posts still require login to Strava

* Angelo has videoconferencing that is available from his home page
* Etherpad makes a document automatically without having to create something
** https://etherpad.indieweb.org/ like we use for IndieWebCamps!
* Jitsi has the same facilities for starting a video call
** https://meet.jit.si
** Warns you if a name is too obvious
** You need to log in to start a room.

* If a URL is not known and not guessable, it is more safe. But as soon as you share it is public.
** Facebook might try and get a meta image

* A password protected page
** Password protected pages don't need Impressums
** The Impressum law actively prevents people from starting websites
** We have https://indieweb.org/Impressum with guidance on the Impressum topic.

* What is good UX that says a post is private
** If auth is required, sharing will not work.
** How do you prevent people from taking screenshots
** Watermarks that would identify part of a page (but could be cropped out?); lesser if the watermark is across the page (but dev tools could edit it out)
** UI: "this is a private post" at the top of my web page
** You assume that you trust the people who have been invited to the page
** Different colour scheme for your private website
** Some kind of emoji
** "Thank you for logging into my website, James"
** Trust is at the centre of all of it
** Share button could say "you can only share X, give direct guidance not to share"

* "Wiki is the slow motion version of Etherpad"

* Private post slugs
** You might not want to put private information in the slug so that it is not identifiable
** A good reason to have a less intuitive slug

* HTTP static
* Use a tag to say "Private"; but public by default would be a problem; could lead to mistakes.
* Slug could say "private"
* How many people understand slugs?

* Cloudflare does a lot of content blocking
** helps you block LLM crawler bots

* Tension between private and discoverability
* How is the way people are accessing information changing?

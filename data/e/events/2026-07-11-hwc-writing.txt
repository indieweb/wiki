== Notes ==
* Welcome to HWC Writing Edition!
* The wounded researcher, researching with soul in mind (https://www.routledge.com/The-Wounded-Researcher-Research-with-Soul-in-Mind/Romanyshyn/p/book/9780367468422)
** Writing project begins with unfinished business that we detect in our lives or in our culture, that feels like it is uniquely ours to bring ourselves to as a practice. Boosting the signal for voices that aren't heard.
* Disciplined forms of writing vs. what we are driven to
** Writing when we should be doing something else, like sleeping or writing, etc.; the moments when nothing is going to happen until the idea is on paper.
* Interesting Q: when did we start writing?
* Having a notebook by your bed to jot down what you are thinking.
* Business card-sized pads that you carry to help you take notes.
* Organising notes when you have them written down -- this is a discrete stage.
* Having an idea in your mind -- a perspective missing in the world -- and jotting it down.
* Some ideas take hold, others don't.
* James jotting down a quote while going down escalators.
* Escalators are robotic stairs, a "painterly image"
* https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tZ8ehplVFp4
* What is the purpose of writing for you?
** Writing = thinking
** Journaling practice
** Chronology as an essential part of blogging -- blog posts have dates
** Academic style; reporting
** Capturing information; remembering
** Keeping lots of notes, some notes make their way to a public website
** Writing to sharpen our own thinking
*** What is my point of view?
*** What is my argument?
*** Personal writing can still have an argument
* Refining arguments as chiselling, like a statue: refining the shape, then the body, then the eyes
* Free writing: writing all your thoughts, then chipping out the bits you don't need
* Writing after a conversation
** Talking about an idea with someone can help you refine an idea
* Every word gets you closer to your goal: a cogent argument, fidelity to an emotion.
* Having a website as a place to put your ideas, even unfinished.
** Sites are venues where you can freely share works-in-progress
* Writing as a way to build upon a conversation
** Not just responding to a single person, but the world
** Writing as signal boosting, but also to add to an argument
* Quoting several people in a single post to bring two authors into a single place; bridging two perspectives.
* The latent potential of curators on the web.
* Another reason to write: we are seized by something, something grabs hold of us, there is a drivenness. Sometimes we write because we don't have a choice, because we must. 
* Writing as an outlet, but being an outlet implies a certain degree of being conscious about going to your notepad to write. Whereas a novel writer might have an impulsive force to explore veins of creativity.
** There can be a feeling of the work not being wholly autonomous.
* Capturing sunsets: if you don't write while the sun is setting, you might miss a detail. The sunset changes every second.
* You can lose track of the words you wanted to use.
* Taylor Swift using voice memos to record songs.
* Does anyone keep a dream journal?
** A few of us do!
* Billie Eilish hums to melodies and then adds the words over time.
** Tabitha: leaving a blank line as a placeholder for later while writing
* "I can't believe it's not poetry" section on Mike's website
* Mike: Prose and programming: both finished before you start in that you have already developed the idea a lot before you get started.
* Mike: an ode to a curl-up bug [add a link]; started in 1993, finished in 2006; it's not always clear to the reader that a work can take so long.
* Word choice in poetry as having a melody, or connecting to a different word in the poem.
* Mike: Good poetry takes an idea and expresses it, like a painting; there is something subtle happening in the creative process. Mike likes rhythm, amusing structure, a different thing.
** Baudelaire, haikus go beyond just the words: a single thought and expression that somehow gets conveyed in the way that it does.
* Q from Tabitha: how do you define when something is finished, either personally or from the perspective of how an audience will receive it?
** We might know something is unfinished, but because it is going to someone, it has to be in its done state.
** When we get older, we may grow into leaving things 80% finished.
** Story about elephant sculpting where you take away everything that isn't an elephant
** Having a WIP tag that is added when there is an obvious flaw / is clearly unfinished; wants to show good ideas but that there is more to an idea than is on the page.
** Combatting perfectionism: when writing about something that is only relevant now (i.e. contemporary), know that if we don't write about it now, it may become irrelevant in a few months to the extent that it wouldn't make as much sense to publish in the future.
** "Unfinishedness" at a project level: doing mini update posts on a project, without the project being completed.
** Writing mini posts as things happen rather than a big compendium.
** Separating the ideas of publishing and finishedness
** James publishes something when he feels happy with it, when it feels true, but he may make minor edits after pressing publish. Studying writing has helped him articulate what the "feeling" is when something is ready, but it is still somewhat illusive.
** Sitting with a blog post overnight.
** Catching typos after publishing :)
** Having the wish to add one more sentence / remove a sentence that doesn't need to be there after a work is published. This is something we can do on blogs / for personal works!
** A piece being finished because it helped me think, not necessarily because it needs to be published.
** Exploratory writing: set aside, but can become a bigger project over time.
* The web allows for different levels of "done-ness"
** A typo in a table of contents in a book is catastrophic in terms of the difficulty to fix after printed
** When something is finished, can open up into layers which is a cool thing.
** Having an internal signal of doneness: having a sense of competion, or I can't look at something because I have been looking at it for too long and needs to be set aside for a bit.
** Done is a relative phenomenon
** Citing websites on accessed on date: allows you to account for the fact the idea will evolve
** Changelogs on websites: shows development of thought.
** A changelog that is implemented as a slider that shows you the development of a post after published.
** Having drafts that have lost personal resonance because they have been in the drafts folder over time.
* A chef knows when something is done not because of a timer, but because of all of their studies.
* If you're looking for Nature writing, it would be hard to find James' site on the web.
* "Audiencelessness"
** To what degree is it okay / helpful to not know your audience / to not have any / much communication with your audience?
** The idea of putting something out there without knowing how it will be received.
** Posting for one's self, without regard for audience. An album not for someone explicitly to like, but exists because we needed to express something.
** There can be immediate feedback when you write fiction online in communities.
** Taking a big risk to do all the creative work for a project upfront.
** A website as an Anthology of self.
** Audience as self vs. audience of others -- where does it overlap
** It's not just the media content of the site, it's also the code, that tells the story
*** A lot of the work coders do on a website can be behind the scenes; the engineering is part of the story.
*** What is the story of our websites?
** Nick: site on public Git repository, which means there is a visible changelog of the writing and the code powering the website.
** https://michaelkupietz.com/devlog/
** By virtue of being who you are, your blog can reflect natural cycles.
*** James noticed that he was able to see when the flower outside bloomed.
*** Everyone would need to discover what this relationship means for them.
** Ana: had Instagram to connect with local creatives, family, etc., but didn't get much feedback / likes; the mix of audience wasn't as interested in that aspect of engaging with the creative writing Ana was publishing.
*** Once you have your website and your blog, there can be people actively looking for specific posts related to a topic you have written about.
** Reading blogs as a foothold to pursuing creatiive interests.
** People who are actively looking for content will find you.
* James: struggling to figure out how to find an audience of more people interested in nature writing.
* Reading older blogs that have large bodies of work that we're still getting through.
** Following someone's journey over time. They might not be maintaining the same blog, but are still creating around how their life is now.
** Interesting to see the progression of someone as a writer, poet over time; connecting the dots to see how someone has found their footing.
** Internet Archive as a means of creating an archive of the web.
** Thomas: never have written for an audience, but have always written things on the top of his head. Always amazed when an idea would take off and get picked up and spread. Not paying attention of who is reading now or who was reading before, just kept rough track of who was responding over time.
*** Currently restructuring archive into collections of posts, trying to avoid problem of adding new articles breaking previous permalinks / not having permanence.
** Peeling back the layers of websites: what dimensions are there? Time, engineering, creative works.
** James often writes posts that have the same title, not intentionally but because a title can resonate for discrete pieces (i.e. he has several posts called Winter, which are differentiated by their publication dates)

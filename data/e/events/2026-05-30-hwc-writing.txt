== Notes ==
* Welcome to HWC Writing!
** For the next 90 minutes, we'll be talking about all things writing on the web!
** Please feel free to add your name to the list of participants at the top of this document.

* What does wandering mean in the context of writing?
** Can have negative connotations: finding the focus to write, finishing drafts
** The other kind of wandering: being free to do something else
** We all have different definitions
** Wandering as part of the Morning Pages practice -- stream of consciousness flows
** When we have time to relax and take a step back, we can wander in a magical way. We can bounce between thoughts: talking about yesterday evening in the next day's Morning Pages, we can go anywhere; talking about the future.
** When we read our stream of consciousness we can reflect deeper, create a blog post.
** Writing can be; it doesn't have to be outcome-focused.
** Sometimes we need to let our minds wander to let creativity hit.
** Wandering relates to getting into a flow state.
*** Thomas captures notes as "blog fodder" which are stubs that can become drafts.
** When you start writing you might have a brain dump as you realise how much you want to write.
* We can get ideas out via talking or typing, but they are two different states / outputs.
** Writing can be how we speak / talk, but that doesn't mean it is merely a transcription of what we would say. We can edit our writing.
* The point comes when we get tired: it might be an ending point, or a break point. If the thought isn't complete / the idea isn't complete, it might be hard to get back into the flow.
** Continuity is tricky. (Can we learn something from reading serial publications, where there may be a multi-month break?)
* Writers can have the habit to take walks and let their thoughts wander.
** Maybe now it is harder to focus when writing than it was before?
* The importance of having a writer's notebook.
** Of avoiding ideas drifting away while we are going about our days.
* Flaneur -- French for wandering around, letting your mind wander, capturing ideas.
** There are books focused on walking and wandering in different cities.
** Walking without a destination is an experience unto itself; you don't necessarily need to have a focal point to come up with ideas.
** Rebecca Solnit has a good book, "A Field Guide to Getting Lost", she also had "Wanderlust: A History of Walking"
* You can use voice-to-text to transcribe as you speak to take notes. Voice Notes too. Apple Watch has facilities too. https://getdrafts.com/ for writing snippets.
* Ideas can be stubs.
* Whiteboards for creative tools.
* When you write something down your mind has something to encourage you to do something with the idea. Noticing that you have ideas is a good thing. Doing something with it is a good thing too.
* What we're working on
** Manny: Blog post about an older-generation Apple Watch that he uses.
** Blog posts as a way to reflect on our relationship with technology; what we use and why, how our thoughts on a piece of technology have changed over time.
** Talking as a way to help us think through an idea.
** Giving ourselves a deadline for a blog post.
** James' trick of writing a blog post down before dinner (i.e. 20-30 minutes before, or while food is cooking)
*** Gives you a specific period of time in which you can write.
* Setting a property in Obsidian to say that a document represents an idea that you want to expand into a blog post.
** General pattern: finding some kind of workflow for documenting ideas / drafts.
* Taking down notes of fun things we have seen / thought -- might make you laugh as you scroll through.
* Structure can give flexibility of doing writing: you know where everything is so you can get to all the notes you need when you need them.
* It can be easy to end up with drafts in multiple different places.
* Potential to do item for writers: going through our drafts and organising them.
* Status: draft, needs edit, published
* Chaotic idea: invite a friend to finish a blog post for you
* Writing while in line -- a way to get some notes down on paper.
* Distancing ourselves from our phone -- trying to write things in a notebook.
** What if we can't find our notebook? (i.e. put it in the other room, didn't bring it with us on a trip)
* What do we use to take notes?
** April: Morning Pages on paper. Wake up, stroll, make tea, pour out ideas. Can be anything.
*** Inspired by descendents of words in different languages. Practicing writing sentences in different languages.
** James: Apple Notes on phone, wherever he is.
* What goes on website may be in notebook first.
* Using glitter pens, stickers. etc to accessorise where we are writing things down.
* Having notebook on desk, bedside.
* Writing where the notebook is as a sacred place / ritual place, just like going to a gym.
* Writing as a restorative practice; once you get into the routine, words can flow.
* There doesn't need to be guilt for missing a routine like Morning Pages; it's about the words.
* Having set spaces can help us stay organised / help us anchor a routine around a place.
** Our mind will do things in the background when we move between spaces.
* Dream notebooks?
* Thomas' idea for a story: writing ideas wherever you are, literally wherever.
* Having different spaces for different kinds of ideas.
** James realised he tends to write more technical things on the whiteboard in his office and more personal things on the whiteboard in his bedroom.
* Gel pens run out quicker than ballpoint pens.
* May be interesting to people here: https://cygnoir.net/analog-tools-meetups
* We can find inspiration in the places we already know.
** "How long has this been happening? When did this start? Why did this start? When did I last see this person? Who was I when I was here last?"
** Walking around like you are viewing something for the first time.
** When we walk with a destination in mind we don't pay attention to what's around us.
* Has someone in Antarctica ever read our work?
* "Twitter ate my blog"
** Twitter provided a new way of communicating with people; short snippets. You don't have to come up with a title: you can write whatever you want. 140, 160, 210 character counts. Encourages concision? (but what about clarity?)
** Having a community is so important; communities are part of our identity.
** When times are hard we need our communities.
* Sharing things for the sake of sharing.
* Constraints can help us with our writing.
* "Eh, I'll push it to next week." -- easy to push our writing back.
* James noticed he can picture a lot of where he was in some of his more creative life writing pieces, but 1-2 line notes are harder to picture. The writing process helps us crystallise ideas.
* Notes are for our future selves.
** https://www.joanwestenberg.com/i-deleted-my-second-brain-692aa40d59d5f06dd5131e43/
* Productivity gurus can promote a particular way of working and people may adopt their systems just because it's what you do if you want to be more productive.
* We hone our writing processes over time. Trying different tools, workflows, creative processes.
** James likes to write everything in a single note in Apple Notes and archive sentences to a different note if they don't feel like they can grow into a blog post.
* Markdown as a format for our writing with rich text that we can bring across lots of different tools.
* "Multiple todays"
** Adding file creation date to title automatically for notes
* Taking notes at conferences, putting tags on files to keep things organised.
* Q: Are old notes from years ago still useful?
** A from Thomas: Quite often they are really helpful, especially for conferences / gatherings. Useful to look up who had an idea.
* Macroscope (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macroscope_(science_concept)): seeing more than what is immediately around and zooming out.
* Sometimes the act of making a note is enough.
* James refers back to his blog posts but not necessarily old notes.
* We might have our own mental bar for from when we backreference (i.e. we may have been writing for five years but only like to refer to things after our first two years of writing).
* Writing process can be to get ideas down without editing.
* Being able to search for things can be really helpful.
* Looking back some of our things can be cringy, but our past works can also be really exciting and interesting.
* https://bowlofmisua.bearblog.dev/coffee-with-my-younger-self/
* Do we look at our old blog posts when we are writing a new post?
** Looking back for posts that we want to link to.
** Things that are ephemeral might not be something we link to as much because there isn't as much of a place.
* Some ideas are really quick, others keep coming back.
** Perennials vs. annuals; gardening.
* Giving ourselves permission to write in a book.
** Sticky notes as a way to take notes in a book without having to write on the book.
* https://www.amazon.co.uk/Digital-Ground-Architecture-Pervasive-Environmental/dp/0262134357

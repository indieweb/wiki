=== Topics Discussed ===

==== What Does 'Balance' Mean In Writing ====

* Balancing what you want to write and read and you think your 'audience' will
** A specific tension here for online writing is considering search engines and how people find your work (pat berlinquette made a good video about this on IG)
** Personal sites often 'rank' for unexpected things, which can sometimes cause pressure on maintaining them
** Writing for oneself is enjoyable and life-giving, but it can often be a lonely endeavor
** Inspiring a written piece by writing to excite, entertain, or provoke a specific person
* Balancing life so one has time for writing in the first place
* Different modes of writing: dedicated consistency for long-term projects otherwise nothing might get done
* Cranking out posts are easier when you harness the initial spark of inspiration
* Monthly dispatches (public diary/confessional) as a motivation to write regularly
* Does publishing on the web affect what you write, especially given attention span?
** Designing for accessible reading experiences can make a huge difference
** Accessible writing doesn't have to mean 'over-simplification' or not experimenting
** Everyone uses the internet differently and there are always outliers (design for the edgecases or the masses?)

==== Organizing Writing On The Web ====

* Creating a graph to show intersections between all categories on one's blog to see if there's a throughline
** What if just the throughline is just the writer themselves? Or their way of seeing the world
** Don't name something after an interest, because what if you want to write about something else?
*** I had this challenge!  I ended up calling my Substack Robertland - the throughline is me!!! 🙂
*** {{tracydurnell}} Had an old blog called Cascadia Inspired for exploring the Pacific Northwest but some of her writing didn't fit that topic so she transitioned to one under her own name
** Blogs often have a theme for a reason other than being a personal hobby
*** A blog as someone's house: most homes don't have a theme, so if it does, it sometimes doesn't feel as lived-in
*  Having guiding principles or a direction vs being boxed-in artifically
* Sharing different aspects of yourself: a blog allows you to do that beautifully
** One part of you might be more interested in technical writing, another might be more keen on art
** A window into your past self in all its complexity through what you were thinking at the time

==== Writing Fiction On One's Blog ====

* Several folks do/want to
* Some people get put off by the seeming enomirty of the task: but it can be super short, a fragment, a flash
* A project {{jo}} is currently working on: a fictionalized journal as an exploratory excersise and a daily-ish writing pratice (available over telnet on port 2002)
* Struggling to commit/finish when beginning something is so easy
* Making fiction writing easier for yourself: having prompts or visual aids for ideas
** A writing prompt {{capjamesg}} recieved: write a museum plaque for a statute, then a 100-word story
* Writing non-fiction seems more 'acceptable/adult/mature' online, so fiction doesn't get done as often
* Writing fiction has unique challenges compared to non-fiction: creating scenarios from scratch than just synthezing and stringing ideas together. It exercises a different part of the brain. As does poetry!
** Recommendation from {{jo}} and Robert: everyone should write fiction/poetry at least one!
* Somehow the internet is more associated with non-fiction so reading fiction in that medium can be harder
* Publishing fiction and groups for writers: email {{zacharykai}} for suggestions! hi@zacharykai.net

==== Interpetability/Context In Writing ===

* Do readers/writers have different expectations of the experience and themselves in fiction vs. non-fiction
** Perhaps there's something inherently more revealing/vulnerable than non-fiction, even personal essays
*** There's an objectivity or at least 'crowdsourced' opinons in non-fiction, there's no chance of hiding behind that in fiction because you have to make everything up from scratch, which says a lot about you.
** The writer has less control over how a fictional pieces is interpreted by a reader which is potentially scary!
*** Does the author's intentions matter at all in fiction? Such is the endless debate!
*** Publishing fiction means giving up control
*** Buying used books often means the books are already annotated, which can be a gift in seeing how others interpret works and how their brains work
*** But really, every piece of writing, genre regardless, is recieved differently by different people
*** How much do you explain to the reader vs letting them decide for themselves? How much can you let go?
**** Poetry especially wrestles with this because you can be as explicit or as vague as you wish
* When you write, then edit, sometimes you find a sentence that could have two meanings, even when you'd only meant it one way. Do you need to clarify it or can you let it be?
* First impressions are best formed without context or critisim, best to only form those on a second reading

===== Ambiguity =====

* Blogging as an excersise in sitting with ambiguity: https://daniel.industries/2025/11/05/ambiguous-work/
** The road can still be clear even if the end isn't, and vice-versa
* Deliberatly making your writing ambiguious forces the reader to not take what you write at face-value
* Attempting to make things as specific and umambigious is often a losing battle because ever
* Complexity and straightforwardness often get misaquated with elitism and accessibility
* Things that ask more questions than they answer seem to have fallen out of favor in pop culture
** 'Content' online seems to 'do better' if there's a cut-and-dry conclusion or hardline opinion
** {{zacharykai}} rants about this to himself in his head endlessly: where has all the nuance gone?
** I'll make sure to bring this up! - James
** It's fascinating how much you can learn if you're willing to step outside your own thoughts
* Is ambiguity at odds with specificity, or do they work together in the learning/thinking process?

==== Writing As Thinking Things Through ====

* Begin with the conclusion of a post and see where it leads you: you might surprise yourself
** You don't have to publish something if it doesn't go anywhere
* Ending a piece of non-fiction online with a question or 'reply via email' button; this post was a response to another blogger's closing questions https://daniel.industries/2025/11/22/why-write-online/
** An 'engagement hack' for the IndieWeb and a way of inciting questions
** It's basically the IndieWeb version of 'like and subscribe!' - the IndieWeb as a republic of letters
** And this often completes the cycle as an email exchange turns into a post!
* Isn't the whole point of writing to continuely find new and interesting questions to ponder?
* Blog posts as conversations and posts inspired by real-world conversations
* Writing as process of consolidation or processing something (in complement or adjacent to conversation)
** Social media or 'the cozy web' or chat/discussions as a generative space or layers for building upon ideas 
* 300-1000 words seems to be the sweet spot for holding people's attention in blog posts
** Editing something down as a courtesy for readers, an act of loving curation
** When everything 'competes' with everything else on the internet, it's not a great reading environment!
** Printing long-form blog posts out or sending them to your e-reader for a better context and focus
*** Allowing folks to export your blog posts as ePUB files to allow for this?
* Your ability to focus on someone's work often correlates with your trust or relationship with them/their art

==== Writing Formats & Length ====

* Do long or short written works facilitate better conversations? Perhaps it depends on the aim
* Where do you end your pieces? How do you end them? 
* {{capjamesg}}'s motto for his writing on his blog is build a bunch of small houses to make a village rather than one mansion
** Essays need construction, blog posts act as their own building blocks
* Some of the earliest blogs were the modern day link blogs or micro blogs (more rabbit-hole beginnings than original thinking or opuses, but blogging 'norms' are always shifting)
* 'Digital gardening' vs 'chronological blogging' as a way of organizing works
** The tendency is almost always additive or stacking more and more
** Perhaps the medium of hypertext encourages this
* Creating context and metadata and labeling systems for your works

==== Writing In Community ====

* Robert wants to join or start a writing club
* Sticking to communities and maintaing them is often the hardest part (structure is the driving force)
** One example could be 'The Echo & Narcissus Writing Club' - copying an existing work's style/themes and then branching out from there with weekly prompts
*** {{daniel}} 's original notes are a published Notion page, but external links appear gone: https://dealingwith.notion.site/Echo-and-Narcissus-Writing-Club-7822fefe606243a199b309432105a6bc
Nicole- Iam interested in joining.  Im trying to write 1k words a day before noon. Perhaps 6am to 8am for myself for next couple years. I'm on DFOS. 
* Hosting events take so much work, and often more than the participants realize. It's hard to maintain these things! We take 'glue' people for granted far too often.
** Event-hosting is an art-form! And a skill that's valuable to learn.
